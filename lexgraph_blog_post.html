Introduction

Why This Dataset, Why Now

Token costs in production RAG systems are not just a budget line — they're a ceiling on what you can build. Every quarter, teams are retrieving more context, hitting rate limits faster, and paying proportionally more for answers that aren't meaningfully better. The industry default, Basic RAG with vector embeddings, has a structural problem: it retrieves similar text, not structurally connected facts.

I wanted to test whether GraphRAG actually solves this on a real, messy, domain-specific dataset. So I built LexGraph — a three-pipeline benchmark running on Indian Supreme Court judgments from the OpenNyai ILDC corpus — for the GraphRAG Inference Hackathon by TigerGraph.

A question like "Which judges expanded Article 21 rights?" requires traversing Judge → Case → Article → Precedent Chain. Four hops. Vector RAG retrieves chunks that mention Article 21. GraphRAG traverses the relationship structure.

Indian Supreme Court judgments are among the most graph-shaped text data in the public domain. Every judgment cites earlier cases. Those cases interpret constitutional articles that have evolved across 70 years of jurisprudence. Judges author dozens of rulings over careers that span decades, building coherent bodies of legal philosophy. This is a dataset where graph structure is not a nice-to-have — it's the only way to answer the most important questions correctly.

For Round 1, I ingested 6,000 cases (~3.8M tokens) from the full 70,000-case corpus. Here's exactly what I built, what worked, what didn't, and what the benchmark data shows.

Graph Schema

The graph models the relational structure of Indian constitutional jurisprudence with five node types and five edge types:

Article Case Act Concept Judge / Bench

Edges: cites · references_article · references_act · authored_by · heard_by

The graph layer uses the TigerGraph GraphRAG repo (Path A), deployed via Docker and queried via REST API. No custom GSQL was needed — the TigerGraph stack handles the traversal entirely.

Context Compression: The Token Reduction Mechanism

# Basic RAG sends raw text chunks
Basic RAG:   query → embed → top-5 chunks (~800 chars each)
             → ~1,500 prompt tokens

# GraphRAG sends structured relationship summaries
GraphRAG:    query → entity extract → graph traversal (3 hops, top-3 nodes)
                  → compress to structured summary (~300 chars per node)
                  → ~500 prompt tokens

Reduction = 1 − (704 / 1,732) ≈ 59.4%

This is what makes GraphRAG economical: graph traversal returns structured relationship data (Case → Article → Judge chains), not raw text dumps. The context is inherently denser because every token carries relational meaning, not padding prose.

Engineering Details

The Five Decisions That Shaped the Results

1. LLM-Based Entity Extraction

The single biggest quality improvement came from replacing regex with an LLM call for entity extraction before every graph traversal. The extractor identifies constitutional article numbers, named cases, acts, legal concepts, judge names, and temporal constraints:

EXTRACT_SYSTEM = """You are a legal entity extractor for Indian SC queries.
Extract all legal entities and return ONLY valid JSON:
{
  "articles": ["21", "14", "19"],
  "cases": ["Maneka Gandhi v Union of India"],
  "acts": ["Prevention of Money Laundering Act"],
  "concepts": ["right to privacy", "basic structure doctrine"],
  "judges": ["Justice Bhagwati", "Justice Krishna Iyer"],
  "temporal": {"after": 2017, "before": null}
}"""

This costs ~100 tokens per query but the traversal precision improvement is significant. Regex fails on Art. 21, Article 21(1), Arts. 14 and 21, and every real-world abbreviation variation. The LLM handles all of them and also extracts temporal filters ("after 2010") and judge names — enabling graph queries that raw embedding search can't support at all.

2. Three-Level Fallback Strategy

GraphRAG tries three context retrieval paths in order, ensuring the pipeline always produces token-efficient output:

def _get_context(self, query, entities):
    # 1. TigerGraph GraphRAG REST service (best case)
    try:
        resp = requests.post(f"{GRAPHRAG_URL}/query",
            json={"query": query, "num_hops": GRAPHRAG_HOPS, "top_k": GRAPHRAG_TOP_K})
        return self._compress(resp.json()["context"])
    except: pass

    # 2. TigerGraph direct GSQL query
    try:
        context = self._tigergraph_direct(query, entities)
        if context: return self._compress(context)
    except: pass

    # 3. Graph-enhanced ChromaDB (entity-enriched, fewer+shorter chunks)
    return self._compress(self._chroma_enhanced(query, entities))

Even the ChromaDB fallback preserves token reduction by enriching the query with extracted entities (higher precision, fewer false-positives), retrieving only 3 chunks instead of 5, and hard-capping each chunk at 300 characters instead of Basic RAG's ~800. This fallback alone delivers ~60–70% token savings.

3. 512-Word Chunk Size

For ChromaDB ingest, chunk size significantly affects both retrieval quality and token consumption. I tested three sizes on Indian legal text:

  • 256 words: Splits case reasoning mid-argument. BERTScore 0.26. Too granular for legal prose.
  • 512 words ✓: Captures paragraph-level legal reasoning. BERTScore 0.31. The sweet spot.
  • 1,024 words: Chunks too broad; top-5 results overwhelm the LLM. BERTScore 0.28.

4. Connection Caching (Easy 2-Second Win)

Early versions created a new TigerGraph connection and token on every query call — adding 1–2 seconds of auth overhead. The fix: initialize once at pipeline startup and cache the connection object. GraphRAG latency dropped from ~5.8s to ~3.8s — making it faster than Basic RAG's 4.3s average.

5. Idempotent Schema Creation

Adding IF NOT EXISTS to every CREATE VERTEX and CREATE EDGE call made the ingest script safe to re-run during development. Schema errors on re-runs cost more debugging time than almost any other issue during a hackathon build.

Benchmark Design

10 Queries Built for Multi-Hop Reasoning

The queries were deliberately designed to maximise GraphRAG's structural advantage — each one requires traversing multiple entity types in sequence, which vector similarity search cannot do.

q01
Which judges most consistently expanded Article 21 rights, and which landmark cases established those precedents?
Judge → Case → Article · 4-hop traversal
q02
How has the right to privacy under Article 21 evolved from the 1950s to the Puttaswamy judgment?
Citation chain across 60 years of precedent
q03
What is the basic structure doctrine and which cases applied it to strike down constitutional amendments?
Kesavananda → downstream citation graph
q07
Trace the citation chain from Maneka Gandhi v. UoI to cases decided after 2010 that relied on it for personal liberty.
Forward citation + temporal filter — pure graph query
q08
Which judges authored the most judgments interpreting both Article 19 and Article 21 together?
Multi-article intersection — impossible with vector search alone
q10
Which constitutional bench decisions on reservation policy cite Indra Sawhney, and how did subsequent judges interpret the 50% ceiling rule?
bench_size filter + citation graph + topic filter · 4 hops with properties

Why Vector RAG Can't Answer These

Query q07 asks for cases that cite a specific case after a specific year for a specific purpose. Vector similarity retrieves documents that mention Maneka Gandhi. Graph traversal follows the cites edge from that node, filters by year > 2010, and returns structurally connected downstream cases. These are fundamentally different operations.

Evaluation Methodology

How the Numbers Were Measured

BERTScore

Each pipeline answer was scored against the ground-truth reference using the bert-score library with microsoft/deberta-xlarge-mnli as the scoring model. DeBERTa outperforms RoBERTa for legal text evaluation because its disentangled attention mechanism better captures entity co-references (judge names, case citations).

Two scores are reported: raw F1 (absolute score against DeBERTa baseline ~0.84) and rescaled F1 (normalized so that random-pair baseline = 0). The rescaled score is the more informative metric — a rescaled score of 0.620 means GraphRAG's answers are 62% better than random on the semantic axis, while Basic RAG is at 31%.

LLM-as-a-Judge

Each answer was graded PASS/FAIL by Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2 (HuggingFace free inference, with Gemini fallback) against the verifiable ground-truth reference. The judge prompt enforces three strict criteria: correct case names and citations, correct legal principle or article interpretation, and no factual hallucinations. This is why LLM-Only scores 0% — its answers are often directionally plausible but unverifiable without corpus grounding.

Retrospective

What I'd Do Differently

Design the graph schema from the queries, not from the data

I started with a generic schema and had to refactor when the multi-article intersection query (q08) needed a dedicated edge. The better approach: write the five hardest queries first, work backward to what graph structure they require, then design the schema. Schema choices constrain everything downstream — the traversal logic, the index structure, and which queries are possible at all.

Path B would push accuracy higher

I used Path A — the TigerGraph GraphRAG repo as-is via REST API. Path B means tuning num_hops, top_k, community_level, and writing custom prompt templates per query type. For legal reasoning, different query categories (citation chains vs. doctrine evolution vs. judge attribution) benefit from different hop depths and retriever configurations. That's the Round 2 priority.

Cache BERTScore model loading

DeBERTa-xlarge-mnli takes 30–60 seconds to download on first call. The first benchmark result gets inflated latency. Add a warm-up call at benchmark start or the first result is an outlier.

Rate limit handling for concurrent tests

Running five parallel queries against Gemini's free tier (15 RPM) hits rate limits immediately. Add exponential backoff retry logic before publishing any throughput numbers, or the p95 latency figures will be dominated by 429 errors rather than actual computation time.

Next Steps

Round 2: Scaling to 70,000 Cases

Round 2 scales from 6,000 to 70,000 cases — roughly a 12× increase to the full ILDC corpus, approximately 50M tokens. The $50 Gemini API credits provided per team enable a full-corpus benchmark that Round 1 couldn't run.

The Round 2 roadmap:

  • Chunk size sensitivity analysis: Test 256, 512, 1,024 tokens at 70k scale. Optimal chunk size may shift with a richer, denser graph.
  • Hop depth sweep: Benchmark GRAPHRAG_HOPS ∈ {2, 3, 4} across accuracy vs. token tradeoff. Deeper hops increase recall but also context size.
  • Retriever selection: Switch from Hybrid Search to Community retriever for broad doctrine queries (q03, q02). Community retrieval is better for "what is the state of X across all cases" queries.
  • Judge-network traversal: The judge_graph.py module implements full judge-network multi-hop (Judge → Case → Citation chain). Enabling it at 70k scale should push BERTScore significantly higher on judge-attribution queries.
  • Chain-of-thought prompts: Adding explicit step-by-step reasoning instructions to the synthesis prompt should raise judge pass rate toward 100% even on ambiguous queries.
Perspective

The Bigger Picture for Indian Legal Tech

Indian legal technology is almost entirely unsolved at the AI layer. Courts publish thousands of judgments per year. Lawyers need precedents. Researchers need citation chains. Law students need case summaries. Litigants need to understand what rights they have and what cases established them.

The knowledge graph connecting 70 years of Supreme Court history already exists in the data — 70,000 judgments, hundreds of thousands of citation relationships, thousands of constitutional article interpretations. It just hasn't been made queryable in a way that preserves the relational structure. Every case knows which articles it interpreted. Every article has a precedent chain stretching back to the Constitution of 1950. Every judge left a body of jurisprudential work that can be traced, compared, and analysed.

GraphRAG makes it queryable. At 59% lower token cost than Basic RAG — with a 2× improvement in semantic accuracy — it makes it economically viable at production scale.

The three-pipeline benchmark in LexGraph is a proof of concept for what becomes possible when you model legal knowledge as a graph rather than a bag of text chunks. The token reduction is the efficiency story. The BERTScore improvement is the accuracy story. But the real story is that questions which were previously unanswerable by AI — trace the citation chain from this 1978 case to all cases after 2010 that cited it for this specific purpose — are now answerable in under 4 seconds.
